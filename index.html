Should You Build Your Own PC?

Building a personal computer, or PC, from scratch is a regular practice done by individuals interested in customizing their machines to their liking. This is done as opposed to purchasing a prebuilt PC, which are computers already assembled by their manufacturers. Before committing to your technological journey, assess yourself. Does building a PC suit your interests? Do you find the potential drawbacks negligible?

Benefits to Building a PC:

  • More customization
  • Cost-efficient
  • Better cooling

Drawbacks to Building a PC:

  • Requires technical knowledge of computer components
  • Takes a learning curve
  • Time-consuming

PC Parts

After deciding whether or not you want to embark on the adventure that is building your own computer, here is an overview of some components. Many of you are already aware of their functions, but included are summaries for those who forgot or weren't knowledgeable in the first place.

Processing

  • Central Processing Unit (CPU) - The "brain" of the computer that performs mathematical calculations.
  • Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) - Processes the computer's graphical outputs.
  • Motherboard - A circuit board that houses computer parts, allowing them to communicate with each other.

Memory

  • Long-term Storage
    • Hard Disk Drive (HDD) - Cheaper, but typically slow and bulky. Because of their low price per gigabyte, they are best suited for budget PC builds or large storage.
    • Solid State Drive (SSD) - More expensive, but faster and lightweight. Best suited for performance and computer longevity, as they last longer on average compared to Hard Disk Drives.
  • Random Access Memory (RAM) - The computer's volatile, short-term memory.

Energy

  • Power Supply Unit (PSU) - Converts the main AC power from the wall outlet to the appropriate DC power used to activate computer components.
  • Heat Sink and Fan (HSF) - Regulates the temperatures of computer parts by blowing out hot air and taking in cool air.
  • Case - Holds all the computer components together.

Input / Output (IO)

  • Keyboard - Communicates the user's key inputs to the computer.
  • Mouse - Translates the user's movement of the device into motions to control the digital cursor on a computer.
  • Monitor - Displays the graphical output of a computer.
  • Speakers - Generates sound from a computer.

Choosing PC Parts: Budgets

After learning about each computer component, it is now time to choose your parts. Price is a crucial factor to not only performance but also aesthetic. For example, a $1,000 PC would perform differently from a $5,000 PC. The reasoning behind this is clear: the better the performance, the more expensive the part. This pattern also applies to the computer's appearance. Having a higher budget paves the way for more customization. With this in mind, choosing the price range for your PC should be off the basis of your interests and needs, such as if you're using it for gaming as opposed to for work. Below is a list of recommended budgets, their capabilities, and who they might appeal to.

  • Budget: $500 - $750
    • May appeal to individuals who are on a tight budget or unconcerned about the performance or aesthetics of their PC.
    • Capable of processing basic computational and graphical tasks.
    • Able to run simple video games, but may encounter issues for more demanding ones.
  • Mid-Range: $750 - $1500
    • May appeal to the average computer user.
    • Able to run more demanding games smoothly.
    • A middle ground between budget and high-end builds.
  • High-End: >$1500
    • May appeal to individuals focused on computer performance.
    • Might interest gamers in need of more powerful components to run video games with higher graphics smoothly.

Choosing PC Parts: Aesthetics

If you are going to build a PC, you might as well make it look nice. Luckily, there are many ways to customize your build. Below are some examples.

  • Following a general color scheme - Since most computer parts come in either black and white, choosing one of the colors to base your build on can create a satisfying color composition.
  • RGB - As the name implies, these gears illuminate your computer with vibrant colors.

Choosing PC Parts: Component Manufacturers

The final portion of this guide explores the actual manufacturing companies behind these computer parts. Some popular brand names are shown in this list. Click the company titles to be redirected to their official websites.

Nvidia

Nvidia specializes in the production of GPUs with their GeForce and Quatro series, which appeal to a wide variety of individuals. From casual users to major corporations, their quality graphics cards are used worldwide.

AMD

AMD manufactures processing technologies like their Ryzen CPUs and Radeon GPUs. As a competitor to Nvidia, their products are also popular worldwide, from everyday computer users to large companies.

Intel

Intel primarily produces CPUs, with their i3, i5, i7, and i9 series. Recently, the company has began manufacturing GPUs with their new Intel Arc graphics cards. Intel and AMD both specialize in processors.

Corsair

Corsair manufactures a plethora of different PC components, including computer cases, power supply units, cooling products such as fans and CPU coolers, gaming mice, keyboards, and headsets; memory sticks, and more.

ASUS

ASUS specializes in monitors, motherboards, and peripherals such as sound cards.

Samsung

Samsung is known for its monitors and storage devices, most notably their SSDs.

Conclusion

Ultimately, PC building is a vast topic. No matter your goal in this journey—whether you want to build a budget computer to get the job done or the lavish, high-end PC of your dreams—we all take the same path in reaching that endpoint through learning. The beauty of this practice is how you have full freedom and autonomy over your creation.With that being said, embrace your creativity and innovation, and happy PC building!
